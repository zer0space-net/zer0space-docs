# May

> The zer0space mascot. She doesn't run any of the nine machines — she watches
> all of them, so somebody else doesn't have to.

![May, character sheet](zer0space-carakterdesigne.png)

---

## Nine machines, one window

May's station has one window, octagonal, too big for the room it's cut into.
Earth turns under it all night — city lights in threads, the rest dark. She
sits cross-legged on the bed with a monitor at her shoulder throwing the only
other light in the room, and watches nine machines the way you'd watch nine
lamps in nine windows of a house you trust: not because anything is expected
to go wrong, but because knowing costs nothing and not knowing costs
everything at the worst possible hour.

Seven of the nine are interchangeable to her on purpose. She couldn't tell you
which one is running what tonight, and that's the point — any of them can die
and the work just starts again somewhere else. The other two she knows by
name. One holds the files. One holds the database. If those ever go quiet,
it's not a lamp flickering, it's the house.

## Nothing she can't lose

She keeps the same rule the cluster does: hold nothing you can't afford to
lose. Whatever she notices doesn't stay with her — it goes into the log, onto
the dashboard, out where the next person on watch can pick it up without
having to ask her first. She likes this about the job. It means she's exactly
as replaceable as everything she's watching, and none of it depends on her
sitting in this exact spot forever.

---

## The corridor that lies

There's a stretch between two of the outer machines that used to check out
green and mean nothing. The light said *reachable*. The traffic said
otherwise — quietly, the way a fault does when it's polite enough not to trip
anything. She learned not to trust the light by itself after that. Now she
watches the second signal too, the one that comes straight from the machines
instead of through the corridor that lied. The corridor's still broken. She
just stopped needing it to be honest.

---

## A door she never opens

She doesn't keep a door open for anyone. When something outside needs in, she
calls out through a line she opened herself and waits for the answer on that
same line — nothing gets to knock. It's a smaller kind of safety than a lock,
and a better one: there's no door to pick if there was never a door.

---

## The watch she keeps honestly

Every night she writes down what actually happened, not what was supposed to.
Most of it is fine — the same seven lamps, the two that matter holding steady,
a small archive stitched together and shipped somewhere else before she lets
herself stop looking. Some of it isn't finished. The far copy she's meant to
keep off-station doesn't exist yet. The database has never once been asked to
prove it can come back from nothing. She doesn't pretend otherwise on the log
— an entry that says *fine* when it isn't is worse than one that says
*not yet*, because the first one is the kind of lie that only gets found at
the worst time.

## So I can sleep

That's the whole job. Nine machines, one honest log, a door that only opens
outward, and a light she no longer trusts by itself. She doesn't need it to be
perfect. She needs to know exactly how imperfect it is, and say so, every
night — so that somewhere else in the building, someone can turn the light
off and actually sleep.
